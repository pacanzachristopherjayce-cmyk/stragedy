The Bath School Disaster

A Calculated Revenge: In the mid-1920s, Andrew Kehoe, a local farmer and the treasurer of the school board in Bath Township, Michigan, became increasingly embittered. He was incensed by a "school tax" levied to pay for the construction of a new consolidated school building, which he blamed for his personal financial struggles and the impending foreclosure of his farm. Kehoe, a talented electrician and "handyman," spent months secretly exploiting his access to the school to plant hundreds of pounds of dynamite and pyrotol (a World War I-era explosive) within the building's infrastructure, meticulously wiring them to a central timing device.

The Day of Horror: On the morning of May 18, 1927, Kehoe began a methodical campaign of destruction. He first murdered his wife, Nellie, and detonated firebombs that leveled his own farm, killing his livestock and destroying his home. At 9:45 AM, an alarm clock triggered the explosives hidden under the north wing of the Bath Consolidated School. The blast reduced the wing to a mountain of rubble, instantly killing dozens of children and teachers. Roughly half an hour later, as rescuers and frantic parents swarmed the site, Kehoe drove up to the school in a truck laden with shrapnel and explosives. He beckoned the school superintendent to his vehicle before firing a rifle into the back seat, detonating the truck and killing himself, the superintendent, and several others nearby.

Key Facts

Date: May 18, 1927

Location: Bath Township, Michigan, United States

Casualties: 45 dead (including 38 children and the perpetrator), at least 58 injured; remains the deadliest mass murder at a school in U.S. history.

Cause: A series of planned explosions perpetrated by a disgruntled school board member using dynamite and pyrotol, fueled by anger over taxes and financial ruin.

Lesson: The Bath School disaster remains the deadliest act of school violence in United States history. It serves as a grim reminder that mass violence is not a modern phenomenon and can stem from deep-seated, obsessive resentment over perceived social or financial injustices. Despite its scale, the event was largely overshadowed in the national media by Charles Lindbergh’s historic transatlantic flight just two days later. Today, it stands as a pivotal case study in the history of domestic terrorism, highlighting the lethal danger of "injustice collectors"—individuals who obsessively harbor grievances until they manifest in catastrophic violence.
