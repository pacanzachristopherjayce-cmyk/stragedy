The Great Molasses Flood

The Afternoon of January 15, 1919: In the North End of Boston, a massive steel tank standing 50 feet tall and holding 2.3 million gallons of fermenting molasses suddenly exploded. This was not a slow leak; it was a structural failure triggered by a rapid rise in temperature from 2°F to 40°F, which increased the internal pressure of the carbon dioxide gas inside the tank. The tank had been built hurriedly by United States Industrial Alcohol (USIA) and had been leaking so badly for years that the company simply painted it brown to hide the cracks.

When the rivets finally popped, a 25 foot high wave of thick, sticky molasses surged through the streets at 35 miles per hour. The sheer weight and density of the liquid—much heavier than water—crushed steel elevated train tracks and leveled entire buildings as if they were made of cardboard. People and horses were trapped in the viscous current, unable to move as the liquid quickly cooled and hardened in the January air. The disaster killed 21 people and injured over 150, many of whom suffocated because they were pinned beneath the weight of the syrup.

From Blaming Sabotage to Legal Accountability: The immediate strategy employed by the USIA was one of Scapegoating. They claimed that "anarchists" had bombed the tank, attempting to frame the tragedy as a terrorist act rather than a failure of their own engineering to avoid paying damages.

Key Facts

Date: January 15, 1919

Location: North End of Boston, Massachusetts

Casualties: 21 deaths and over 150 injuries

Cause: Engineering and Structural Negligence (Explosion of a 2.3 million gallon molasses tank due to pressure and metal fatigue)

The Litigation Strategy: The resulting legal battle lasted six years and involved over 1,000 witnesses. It was the first time in American history that "Expert Witnesses"—engineers and architects—were brought in to testify about structural integrity and metal fatigue.

The Legislative Goal: The strategy of the plaintiffs was to prove that the company had skipped basic safety tests. Their victory led to a massive shift in corporate accountability, resulting in the creation of Building Codes and Professional Licensing across the United States.

The Great Molasses Flood is a bizarre but brutal reminder of "Administrative Negligence." It teaches us that even the most harmless substance can become a deadly weapon when stored without proper engineering oversight. The strategy of shifting the blame to "outside agitators" failed, ultimately leading to the modern safety standards that govern every building we enter today.
