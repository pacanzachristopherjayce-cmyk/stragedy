THE TSUNAMI OF LISBON

The Morning of November 1, 1755 On All Saints' Day, while most of the population was crowded into stone churches for mass, a massive earthquake (estimated at 8.5 to 9.0 on the Richter scale) struck the Atlantic floor off the coast of Portugal. The shaking lasted for an agonizing six minutes, opening fissures five meters wide in the city center and toppling the heavy stone cathedrals onto the congregations. The tragedy unfolded in three distinct waves. First, the earthquake leveled the city. Second, as survivors rushed to the open docks to escape falling buildings and the resulting firestorm (caused by thousands of overturned church candles), they noticed the tide had pulled back further than anyone had ever seen, revealing shipwrecks and lost cargo. Roughly 40 minutes later, a 40-foot-high tsunami roared into the harbor, swallowing the docks and everyone on them. Between the tremors, the fires, and the water, approximately 60,000 to 100,000 people were killed, and 85% of Lisbon's buildings were erased.
