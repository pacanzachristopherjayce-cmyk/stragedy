The Tsunami of Lisbon

The Morning of November 1, 1755 On All Saints' Day, while most of the population was crowded into stone churches for mass, a massive earthquake (estimated at 8.5 to 9.0 on the Richter scale) struck the Atlantic floor off the coast of Portugal. The shaking lasted for an agonizing six minutes, opening fissures five meters wide in the city center and toppling the heavy stone cathedrals onto the congregations. The tragedy unfolded in three distinct waves. First, the earthquake leveled the city. Second, as survivors rushed to the open docks to escape falling buildings and the resulting firestorm (caused by thousands of overturned church candles), they noticed the tide had pulled back further than anyone had ever seen, revealing shipwrecks and lost cargo. Roughly 40 minutes later, a 40-foot-high tsunami roared into the harbor, swallowing the docks and everyone on them. Between the tremors, the fires, and the water, approximately 60,000 to 100,000 people were killed, and 85% of Lisbon's buildings were erased.

The Strategic Response: The Birth of Disaster Management The immediate strategy by the Prime Minister, the Marquis of Pombal, is considered the first instance of modern Disaster Recovery Planning. He famously said, "Bury the dead and feed the living."

The Urban Strategy: Pombal ignored the old medieval layout and rebuilt Lisbon with wide avenues and "Seismic-Resistant" buildings. He tested these new designs by having soldiers march in rhythm around wooden models to simulate earthquake tremors. This event shifted the global human strategy from "Theological Acceptance" to Seismology. For the first time, the state sent out questionnaires to every parish to record the exact time, duration, and effects of the quake, creating the first systematic database of seismic activity.

Key Facts

Date: November 1, 1755

Location: Lisbon, Portugal

Casualties: 60,000 to 100,000 deaths

Cause: Tectonic / Multi-Hazard Event (Magnitude 8.5–9.0 earthquake triggering a massive tsunami and city-wide firestorms)

The Philosophical Goal: The horror of the event led thinkers like Voltaire to challenge the "Optimism" strategy of the era, arguing that such tragedies proved that nature was indifferent to human morality.

The Aftermath and Recovery In the wake of the disaster, the Marquis of Pombal implemented a series of reforms aimed at rebuilding the city and improving its infrastructure. He established new building codes to ensure that structures could withstand future earthquakes and initiated a comprehensive urban planning strategy. This response not only helped Lisbon recover but also set a precedent for disaster management worldwide.
