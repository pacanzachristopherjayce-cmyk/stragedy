Title

The Morning of April 24, 2013 In Savar, Bangladesh, the Rana Plaza—an eight-story commercial building housing five garment factories—suddenly imploded during the morning rush. The day before, large structural cracks had appeared in the walls, causing a panic. While the shops and a bank on the lower floors immediately closed, the factory owners upstairs ordered their 3,000+ employees to return to work under threat of losing a month's pay.

/>

The tragedy was a result of illegal "Vertical Expansion." The building was originally designed for shops and offices, not heavy industrial machinery. The upper four floors were added without permits, and the structural pillars were never reinforced to handle the constant vibration of thousands of industrial sewing machines and massive power generators. When those generators were switched on during a power outage that morning, the resulting vibrations acted like a localized earthquake. The building pancaked in less than 90 seconds, killing 1,134 people and injuring over 2,500 more. It remains the deadliest structural failure accident in modern history.

Strategic Response: Corporate Denial to Global Accountability The immediate strategy of the global brands sourcing from these factories was Plausible Deniability. Many companies initially claimed they had no record of producing clothes at Rana Plaza due to a complex web of unauthorized subcontracting.

Key Facts

Date: April 24, 2013

Location: Savar, Bangladesh

Casualties: 1,134 deaths and over 2,500 injuries

Cause: Structural Failure and Regulatory Negligence (Illegal vertical expansion and heavy industrial vibrations in a building designed for light commercial use)
